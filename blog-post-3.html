The Outpost Masterclass: Karpov vs Kasparov 1985 (Game 16)

AP

GM Alex Petrov

Senior Coach

Game 16 of the 1985 World Chess Championship between Anatoly Karpov and Garry Kasparov is widely regarded as one of the greatest strategic masterpieces of positional chess. In this game, Kasparov introduced a revolutionary concept of piece domination using a deep outpost square.

Playing Black, Kasparov chose a highly dynamic Sicilian Defense. Following Karpov's somewhat passive development plan, Kasparov struck with an early pawn sacrifice and on move 16, planted his knight on the d3 square: 16... Nd3!. This knight came to be known in chess lore as the "octopus" knight.

"Grandmaster Raymond Keene famously described Kasparov's d3 knight as an octopus because it stretched its tentacles deep into White's camp, controlling c1, e1, f2, b2, and completely paralyzing White's rook and queen coordination."
Visualizing Karpov's defensive layout and the legendary d3 outpost.

The knight remained a dominant force for 18 moves, making it impossible for Karpov to generate any counterplay. Finally, on move 34, Karpov was forced to sacrifice his queen (34. Qxd3) to remove the octopus knight. Kasparov immediately followed up with the tactical blow 34... Nf2+!, winning the queen and securing a decisive advantage that let him win the game and ultimately take the lead in the historic match.

Key Strategic Takeaways

  • Outpost Domination: Planting a piece (especially a knight) deep inside the opponent's territory, supported by a pawn and unable to be attacked by enemy pawns, can paralyze their entire army.
  • Positional Sacrifice: Kasparov sacrificed a pawn in the center to open diagonals and secure the d3 square for his knight. Activity is often worth more than material.
  • Patience and Restriction: Once the outpost is established, do not rush. Restrict the opponent's moves slowly and grind down their structure.
